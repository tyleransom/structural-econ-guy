\begin{frame}

Review: Linear just-identified IV

\bigskip

Two equations:
\begin{align*}
Y &= X\beta + u \\
X &= Z\pi + \nu
\end{align*}

\onslide<2->{
Two key assumptions for IV validity:

\bigskip

\begin{itemize}
\itemsep1.5em
\item<3-> \textcolor{navy}{Instrument relevance}: $\text{Cov}(Z,X) \neq 0$ (strong first stage)

\item<4-> \textcolor{navy}{Exclusion restriction}: $\text{Cov}(Z,u) = 0$ (instrument only affects $Y$ through $X$)
\end{itemize}
}

\onslide<5->{
\bigskip

Recent literature has raised the bar for what counts as ``plausible''
}

\end{frame}




\begin{frame}

Three critical papers in early 2020s:
\bigskip\par
\begin{enumerate}
\itemsep1.5em
\item<2-> Mellon (2025, \textit{AJPS}): rainfall used as IV in many papers $\Rightarrow$ violates exclusion restriction

\item<3-> Lee et al. (2022, \textit{AER}): valid inference requires $F > 105$ rather than $F > 10$

\item<4-> Young (2022, \textit{EER}): AEA papers show IV results highly sensitive to outliers

\end{enumerate}

\end{frame}

\begin{frame}

\only<3>{\includegraphics[width=0.96\textwidth]{MellonDAG.png}}


Exclusion restriction violations

\bigskip

\begin{itemize}
\itemsep1.5em
\item<2,4-> Mellon (2025) documents \textcolor{navy}{192 papers} using weather as IV

\item<4-> Each pathway represents a potential exclusion-restriction violation

\item<5-> For weather IVs, scholars must assume \textcolor{navy}{hundreds} of additional identifying assumptions

\item<6-> Similar concerns likely apply to other commonly-used instruments
\end{itemize}

\end{frame}

\begin{frame}

Problems in IV when using $t$-test for $H_0: \beta = 0$:

\bigskip

\begin{itemize}
\itemsep1.5em
\item<2-> Stock and Yogo (2005): $F > 10$ became standard pre-test for IV relevance

\item<3-> Lee et al. (2022): conventional $t$-ratio over-rejects \textcolor{navy}{even with $F > 10$}

\item<4-> Introduce \textcolor{navy}{$tF$ procedure}: adjust critical values for 2nd-stage $t$ based on 1st-stage $F$

\item<5-> For 25\% of AER specifications, corrected standard errors \textcolor{navy}{49\% larger} at 5\% level
\end{itemize}

\end{frame}

\begin{frame}

Statistical power in practice

\bigskip

\begin{itemize}
\itemsep1.5em
\item<2-> Young (2022): IV has \textcolor{navy}{little power} in published work

\item<3-> Despite producing different estimates, IV rarely rejects OLS point estimate

\item<4-> Rarely rejects null that OLS is unbiased

\item<5-> Non-\textit{iid} errors and high leverage worsen both size and power
\end{itemize}

\end{frame}

\begin{frame}

Sensitivity to observations

\bigskip

\begin{itemize}
\itemsep1.5em
\item<2-> Young (2022): with deletion of \textcolor{navy}{one} cluster, 39\% of significant results become insignificant

\item<3-> With deletion of \textcolor{navy}{two} clusters, 58\% become insignificant

\item<4-> First-stage $F$-statistics similarly sensitive to outliers

\item<5-> Problems concentrated in high-leverage specifications
\end{itemize}

\end{frame}


\begin{frame}

Given these limitations, maybe we shouldn't do IV?

\bigskip

\onslide<2->{And if we're not going to do IV, what else can we do?}

\bigskip

\begin{itemize}
\itemsep1.5em
\item<3-> Do OLS with lots and lots of covariates to eliminate OVB

\item<4-> Walk away and admit that it's impossible to obtain a causal estimate

\item<5-> Use \textcolor{navy}{partial identification} methods to put bounds on causal effects:
\bigskip\par
    \begin{itemize}
    \itemsep1.5em
    \item<6-> ``Upper'' bound: biased OLS
    \item<7-> ``Lower'' bound: plausible treatment effect
    \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}

\end{frame}
